part 1

The year is 1941. In the heart of Krakow's Jewish Quarter, four lives are intertwined. Sarah, a gifted violinist, finds solace in her music, a stark contrast to the growing darkness outside. Her younger brother, Samuel, is a bookish and gentle soul, his world defined by the stories in his worn-out copy of "The Little Prince." Their best friend, Elara, is a fiercely independent and pragmatic girl, skilled in sewing and a master of observation, always a step ahead of the impending doom. Their close friend, David, is a blacksmith's apprentice, strong and quiet, his hands calloused from work but his heart full of loyalty.

part 2

Life in the ghetto begins with restrictions, a slow tightening of the noose. The vibrant streets turn grey. One evening, a friend of their parents, a tailor named Mendel, gives Elara a peculiar gift: a small, intricately carved wooden bird. He explains it's a family heirloom, a symbol of hope and freedom. Elara, with her pragmatic nature, tucks it away, not fully understanding its significance.

part 3

As the situation worsens, the four friends find themselves separated. Sarah and Samuel are taken to Auschwitz. In the camp, Sarah's violin is confiscated, but she manages to hide a small, embroidered handkerchief her mother had given her, a token of comfort. Samuel, clinging to his book, rips out a single page with a drawing of a rose, believing it will remind him of the beauty he once knew.

part 4

Meanwhile, Elara and David are sent to a forced labor camp near a factory. Elara, with her sewing skills, is put to work mending uniforms. She keeps the wooden bird hidden in the lining of her coat, its presence a secret source of strength. David, with his strong hands, is forced to work in the camp's smithy. He secretly forges a small, simple ring from a piece of scrap metal he finds, a vow to himself that he will find Elara and the others again.

part 5

The war rages on, and they endure unspeakable horrors, their shared memories a fragile lifeline. Sarah and Samuel, through a stroke of luck and the kindness of an older prisoner, are able to communicate a few times with Elara and David, through a smuggled note. In this note, they mention the handkerchief and the page from the book, giving them a glimmer of hope that they are still holding on.

part 6

Years pass. The war ends, but the world they knew is gone. Elara and David, both survivors, are reunited in a displaced persons camp. David has the forged ring, and Elara still has the wooden bird. They hold onto these objects, fragments of their past, as they search for news of Sarah and Samuel. After months of searching, they receive the devastating news that Sarah and Samuel did not survive.

part 7

Their shared grief is immense, but in their hands, the objects—the bird and the ring—become more than just keepsakes. They are a promise to remember, a testament to the lives that were, and a foundation for the lives they must now build. Elara takes the wooden bird out of her coat, and David slips the forged ring onto her finger. The objects, once tokens of a life interrupted, now signify the beginning of a new one, built on the memory of their lost friends.
